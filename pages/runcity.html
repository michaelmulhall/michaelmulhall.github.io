Run City
Product design


Running through a city allows people to appreciate local neighborhoods, beautiful parks, and popular landmarks, but also delivers challenges — crime, surface conditions, and traffic. Along with three other students, I designed Run City — an app that provides runners with safe, convenient, and personalized routes and encourages exploring the city with the running community.

The Users:

City runners:

People who primarily run in urban areas. We selected city runners as our user group because we wanted to design something close to life but also explore a user group we were unfamiliar with—none of our team members are runners.


A familiar problem:

Runners are reluctant to try new routes, especially if it means sacrificing convenience, feasibility, and safety.



The solution:

Runners can create personalized routes with comprehensive safety options, log details and reflections about their run, and interact with the local running community by joining events and posting route reviews.



Filters:

Prioritizing Runners' Safety

The preferences page includes filters for excluding areas with poor lighting, busy intersections, and surfaces that might lead to injury.

Route Details:

Relevant & Convenient Routes

Runners can use detailed route information and user ratings and reviews to find their perfect run.

Performance:

All-In-One Navigation

Route navigation is designed give users all the information they need - detailed directions, metrics like calorie count, and a 911 button for emergencies.

Research:

How can we provide relevant and useful information to city runners to improve their outdoor running experience?

We conducted six semi-structured interviews to better understand urban runners and find opportunities for design. We also participated in an immersion activity to develop empathy for our users. These were then synthesized into personas and a user journey map to better understand user painpoints.


Our Main Findings:

Safety is significant to some runners

Running in a group, steering clear of unsafe areas, and avoiding running in the dark all increase safety.


Runners are reluctant to try new routes

Especially if it means sacrificing convenience, feasibility, and safety.


Runners usually bring few items

They want to focus on the run itself and be distraction-free.



Design Requirements

Using our research findings, we developed several design requirements to help us solve our users' primary pain points.

01 Help runners find routes based on personal preferences

02 Allow for runners to share information with the running community

03 Allow runners to track their running performance

04 Allow runners to explore and learn more about their city

Evaluate:

Usability Testing Takeaways:

01 Users had difficulty interpreting some of the icons and buttons with the corresponding functionality

02 Users found the layout of certain screens hard to navigate and unclear

03 Runners did not want to take out their phones during the run and expressed a desire for expanded audio cues.

Using the feedback from the evaluation testing, we made wireframes for each page in our mobile application. We incorporated suggestions, such as making icons less ambiguous with labels and better visuals.


Task 1: Rate and tag a finished run



Task 2: Find a running route using preferences



Task 3: Use the community feed to join a group's running event





Final mockups:

After several rounds of iterations, we created the high-fidelity version of our app. We wanted our visual design to be clean and simple so users of different ages and tech-savviness could use it with ease. Our color theme was inspired by the sunset/sunrise that many of our users see while running.

Reflection:

What I learned

During the period of ideation, we were sometimes overwhelmed by all of the possible approaches we could take. The design requirements also took a great amount of thoughtfulness; sometimes the simplest ideas are the toughest.
We also noticed the interface designs took longer than expected; in a truly collaboratory fashion, even a simple icon switch could generate long discussions.



What would I do differently?

I recently revisited this project while rebuilding my portfolio. As I worked my way through this case study, I noticed a few aspects that I would do differently.
Although we did try to narrow down what pain points we were targeting, we still tried to tackle too much in a single app. Looking back, I think we would have a stronger product if we only focused on safety or only focused on exploring cities.
I also noticed some issues regarding small text and buttons. Users cannot interact with a button if the touch target size is too small.
Although this was my very first UX project, I believe I succeeded in delivering a polished, usable product that meets the needs of a specific user group.
