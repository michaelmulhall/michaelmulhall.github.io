Happy Plate
Product design


This cooking app mockup is a redesign of a group project. The premise of the app was provided as a teaching vehicle for various methods of user research, creating wireframes, prototypes and to promote team work.



Timeframe:
09/2019 - 11/2019

Tools:
Figma, Illustrator, Principle

Deliverables:
User Research, Wireframes, Hi-fi Prototype

Platform:
Mobile

A familiar problem:

Students develop unhealthy eating habits that can affect physical and mental wellbeing.

Food is an aspect of living that every university student has in common. Although eating is a crucial part of life, many students find themselves unfamiliar in the kitchen after moving away from home.

This has become a significant problem, as many students develop unhealthy eating habits that can affect physical and mental wellbeing. Those who don’t cook at home for themselves often resort to eating out or eating prepared frozen meals, which isn’t cost effective in maintaining a student budget. It does not provide the nutritional value nor cover all food groups that well planned home cooked meals do.

Illustration drawn by me using Procreate



The solution:

I wanted the app to be able to teach students how to cook for themselves. Introduce new healthy meal ideas and how to integrate nutrition into student diets. Introduce low budget meal ideas, and prevent students from resorting to eating out.

What I learned:

Consistency using design systems

Crafting the visual interface meant I was constantly implementing tiny changes throughout the entire prototype. Exploring how to create a design system really saved me countless hours and also made it much easier to establish consistency, whether that be with defining margins, font styles, or shadows.



Deconstruct what’s been done and improve on it

In the beginning, I wanted to create something unlike anything else. However, I soon realized this isn’t feasible nor practical. I turned my efforts to breaking down existing designs (a lot of inspiration from Facebook, Apple Photos, and Dribbble) and analyzing why it works, why it doesn’t work, and what I can do to take it a step further.



Detach bias and turn feedback into opportunities

Interpreting user tests showed me the importance of understanding my own bias and instead, to let the research and testing guide the decision making. This allowed me to turn feedback into opportunities. If I didn’t do this, I would not have thought of adding a recommendations page, nor would I have separated it from the rest of the events.
